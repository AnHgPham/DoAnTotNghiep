Abstract
This study presents an enhanced evaluation framework for few-shot open-set keyword spot-
ting (KWS) systems, addressing critical limitations in existing testing protocols. Building
upon the unified pipeline framework introduced by Rusci et al., we propose three funda-
mental improvements to evaluation methodology: (1) direct L2 distance-based classifica-
tion that eliminates artificial probability normalization constraints, (2) comprehensive mean
Detection Error Tradeoff (DET) curve analysis that captures per-class performance charac-
teristics, and (3) enhanced data utilization strategies incorporating randomized word selec-
tion and expanded vocabulary through the Multilingual Spoken Words Corpus (MSWC).
Our experimental validation demonstrates that direct distance-based classification achieves
superior performance compared to probability-based approaches, with improvements of
0.01 in AUC, 0.05 in accuracy at 5% false acceptance rate, and 0.04 reduction in false re-
jection rate. The proposed evaluation framework reveals significant performance variations
across different data utilization strategies, with fixed word partitions showing artificially
optimistic results compared to randomized selections that better simulate real-world de-
ployment scenarios. Few-shot performance scaling analysis indicates diminishing returns
beyond 5-shot enrollment, with performance improvements plateauing at higher sample
counts.
The enhanced testing protocol provides more rigorous assessment of model generalizability
and offers clearer insights into system behavior through intuitive threshold interpretation.
These contributions establish a more reliable foundation for evaluating few-shot open-set
KWS systems and facilitate better comparison of different approaches in the literature.
The methodology is validated across multiple experimental configurations, demonstrating
its effectiveness and practical applicability for advancing keyword spotting research.
Keywords: Keyword Spotting, On-Device Customization, Metric Learning, Prototypical
Network, Open-set Classification, Evaluation Protocol.

1.
Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
Keyword Spotting (KWS) represents a fundamental challenge in speech processing where
systems must automatically detect the occurrence of predefined target keywords or phrases
within continuous audio streams. This technology serves as the backbone for numerous
real-world applications, including voice-activated assistants such as Amazon Alexa and
Google Assistant, hands-free control systems in automotive environments, and smart home
devices that respond to wake words like ”Hey Siri” or ”OK Google.” The everywhere pres-
ence of KWS in modern human-computer interaction underscores its critical importance in
creating intuitive and accessible interfaces across diverse technological platforms.
While KWS is fundamentally a detection problem that requires localizing keywords within
continuous audio streams, the practical implementation typically transforms this challenge
into a real-time classification task. This transformation occurs through a windowing ap-
proach where the continuous audio stream is segmented into short, overlapping windows,
typically lasting one second in duration [2]. Each window is then processed independently
by a classification model that determines whether the target keyword is present within. The
scope of our study is primarily focused on the quality of the classification model, leaving
out concerns regarding the handling of continuous classification outputs.
The increasing demand for personalized and adaptable voice interfaces has revealed a criti-
cal limitation in traditional keyword spotting (KWS) systems: their inability to rapidly cus-
tomize target keywords without extensive retraining. This challenge becomes particularly
acute in scenarios where users need to define custom wake words or where applications
must adapt to domain-specific terminology. To address this limitation, researchers have
increasingly explored few-shot learning approaches, with Prototypical Networks emerg-
ing as a particularly promising framework. Prototypical Networks allow KWS systems
to learn new keywords from just a handful of examples by constructing prototype repre-
1


=== PAGE 8 ===
sentations in an embedding space, enabling rapid adaptation to new vocabulary without
the computational overhead of full model retraining. An essential feature for any KWS
system is open-set classification, namely the ability to respond with ”I don’t know” when
encountering audio segments that do not contain any of the target keywords. This open-set
recognition capability is crucial for preventing false positives and ensuring that systems
respond only to valid keyword utterances rather than irrelevant speech.
Recognizing that the current literature on few-shot open-set KWS remains highly frag-
mented and lacks clear design guidelines for on-device customization, Rusci et al. [4]
proposed a unified evaluation framework for such problem. Furthermore, they introduced
a novel training strategy that uses different datasets for the training and testing phases, a
design choice that more accurately reflects real-world deployment conditions and enables
a more rigorous assessment of model generalization.
1.2
Objectives
Building upon the unified pipeline framework introduced by Rusci et al. [4], this study
critically examines their testing phase protocol, with a particular focus on data utilization
strategies, score computation methodologies, and evaluation metrics employed in few-shot
open-set keyword spotting (KWS) systems.
To address these concerns and contribute to more rigorous evaluation standards, this study
aims to:
• Perform a comprehensive analysis of the existing testing protocol, assessing data se-
lection procedures, score computation algorithms, and performance metrics, in order
to identify potential limitations that may compromise the reliability and interpretabil-
ity of experimental results in few-shot open-set KWS.
• Design and propose an enhanced testing protocol that mitigates the identified short-
comings, explicitly tailored to address the dual challenges of few-shot learning and
open-set recognition in KWS applications.
• Conduct experimental validation of the proposed testing protocol under various con-
figurations to demonstrate its effectiveness and practical applicability.

2.1
Prototypical Network
2.1.1
The Core Idea
The prototypical networks framework, introduced in [5], represents a foundational paradigm
shift in how we approach classification problems with limited training data. To understand
its significance in the context of keyword spotting, we must first appreciate the fundamen-
tal challenge it addresses: how can a system learn to classify new categories when only a
handful of examples are available for each category?
The core insight of prototypical networks lies in learning a metric space where classifica-
tion can be performed by computing distances to prototype representations. Rather than
learning to directly predict class labels through traditional softmax classifiers, the system
learns to embed input samples into a feature space where samples from the same class
cluster together, while samples from different classes remain well-separated.
Think of this approach as learning to create a “representative fingerprint” for each class.
During training, the network learns how to extract meaningful features from input sam-
ples and combine multiple examples of the same class into a single prototype vector that
captures the essence of that class. This prototype serves as an anchor point in the learned
feature space, representing what makes that particular class distinctive.
The mathematical elegance of this approach becomes apparent when we consider how clas-
sification decisions are made. Given a new query sample, the system embeds it into the
same learned feature space and computes its distance to each class prototype (see figure
2.1). The classification decision is then based on the nearest prototype, following the intu-
itive principle that similar samples should have similar representations.

2.1.2
Training Protocol with Episodic Batching
The training protocol for prototypical networks fundamentally differs from conventional
supervised learning approaches.
Instead of processing data in traditional mini-batches
where samples from all classes are mixed together, prototypical networks employ an episodic
training strategy that explicitly mimics the few-shot learning scenario the system will en-
counter during deployment.
The episodic training paradigm is designed to create training episodes that mirror the struc-
ture of few-shot learning tasks. Each episode can be thought of as a miniature learning
problem where the model must quickly adapt to classify a small set of novel classes based
on only a few examples per class. This approach ensures that the feature encoder learns
representations that are immediately useful for prototype-based classification, rather than
5


=== PAGE 12 ===
learning features optimized for a large fixed vocabulary.
During each training episode, we construct what is known as an N-way K-shot classifica-
tion task. Here, N represents the number of classes to be distinguished within the episode,
while K indicates the number of support examples available for each class. The episode
construction process begins by randomly sampling N classes from the training dataset, en-
suring these classes are different from those used in previous episodes within the same
training batch.
For each selected class, we then randomly sample K examples to form the support set,
along with additional query examples that will be used for computing the loss. The support
examples serve the dual purpose of computing class prototypes and providing the context
for classification, while the query examples represent the samples that must be classified
using the learned prototypes.
The mathematical formulation of an episode can be expressed as follows. Let Dtrain repre-
sent our training dataset containing many classes. For each episode e, we sample a subset
of classes Ce ⊂Dtrain where |Ce| = N. For each class c ∈Ce, we sample K support exam-
ples and Q query examples, where Q is typically set to ensure sufficient gradient signal for
training.

Furthermore, the episodic approach naturally incorporates the variability inherent in few-

The choice of N and K during training deserves careful consideration, as it directly impacts
the learned representations. Training with larger values of N encourages the network to
learn more discriminative features that can distinguish between many classes simultane-

mally informative features from minimal data, which is crucial for few-shot performance.

2.2.1
System Workflow
The KWS system proposed by [4] follows a two-stage pipeline that separates the computa-
tionally intensive training phase from the lightweight deployment phase (figure 2.2). The
overall workflow consists of the following key components:

source dataset (Multilingual Spoken Words Corpus) using episodic training protocols. The
encoder learns to map audio inputs to a discriminative embedding space where samples
from the same keyword class cluster together while different classes remain well-separated.
Enrollment Phase: During deployment, users provide a small number of enrollment sam-
ples (typically 1-10 samples) for each desired keyword. These samples are processed
through the pre-trained frozen feature encoder to generate embeddings, which are then

2.2.3.4
Best Configuration and Key Findings
The experimental results reveal several important findings:
Optimal Configuration: The best performing setup consists of DSCNN-L with normal-
ized features (NORM) trained using triplet loss and paired with openNCM classifier, achiev-
ing 76% accuracy in 10-shot scenarios while maintaining 5% false acceptance rate.
Training Loss Comparison: Triplet loss significantly outperforms both prototypical net-
works (PN) and angular prototypical (AP) variants, showing 8-10% accuracy improve-
ments on DSCNN-L configurations.
Feature Normalization Impact: L2 normalization proves crucial for triplet loss effective-
ness, with DSCNN-L-NORM achieving 4-5% accuracy improvements over CONV and
RELU variants when trained with triplet loss.
Open-Set Classifier Performance: openNCM consistently achieves the highest accura-
cies across different configurations, while OpenMAX shows poor performance in few-shot
scenarios due to insufficient samples for reliable statistical modeling.
These findings establish triplet loss with normalized DSCNN features and openNCM clas-
sification as the preferred configuration for few-shot open-set keyword spotting systems.

2.4.1
Confusion Matrix
A confusion matrix is a standard tool for visualizing the performance of a classification
model by comparing predicted outcomes against ground-truth labels.
For binary classification (positive vs. negative classes), the confusion matrix consists of
four elements:
• True Positive (TP): Number of positive samples correctly identified as positive.
• False Positive (FP): Number of negative samples incorrectly identified as positive.
• True Negative (TN): Number of negative samples correctly identified as negative.
• False Negative (FN): Number of positive samples incorrectly identified as negative.
In the multi-class case of KWS, the confusion matrix can be decomposed into multiple
binary matrices by treating each class as positive while considering the others as negative.
Standard binary metrics can then be computed per class and averaged (macro- or micro-
averaging) to provide an overall performance measure.

AUC
The Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) is a widely used metric for evaluating the overall
performance of a binary classifier. The ROC curve plots the TPR against the FPR across
different decision thresholds.
Since each classification is associated with a confidence score, the decision threshold de-
termines the minimum score required to classify a sample as positive. A higher threshold

2.4.3

3.
Methodology
The primary contribution of this study lies in providing an enhanced version of the testing
protocol originally proposed by Rusci et al. [4] for few-shot open-set keyword spotting
systems. Our improvements address three fundamental aspects of their evaluation frame-
work: data utilization strategies, prediction score computation methods, and performance
metrics selection. These enhancements focus exclusively on the enrollment and inference
phases of the system, treating the feature encoder as a frozen function that transforms audio
signals into embedding vectors, thereby maintaining consistency with the few-shot learning
paradigm.
3.1
Analysis of the Original Approach
Before presenting our proposed improvements, we first provide a comprehensive analysis
of the existing methodology to establish the foundation for our enhancements. Under-
standing the strengths and limitations of the current approach is essential for appreciating
the necessity and impact of our proposed modifications.
3.1.1
Data Utilization Strategy
Rusci et al. [4] established a systematic approach to data partitioning using the Google
Speech Commands (GSC) dataset. Their methodology involves creating two distinct par-
titions from the 35 available word classes. The positive partition, denoted as GSC+, com-
prises 10 target keyword classes (yes, no, up, down, left, right, on, off, stop, go), which
represent the words that the system should recognize and classify correctly. The negative
partition, GSC-, contains the remaining 20 word classes, with a notable exception of 5
specific words (backward, forward, visual, follow, learn) that are excluded entirely from
the evaluation protocol. These excluded words serve a specialized purpose in computing
21


=== PAGE 28 ===
unknown prototypes when using the openNCM classifier architecture.
The original approach leverages the existing train-development-test split structure of the
GSC dataset, which follows a hash function-based partitioning scheme applied to file-
names, resulting in an 8:1:1 ratio across the three splits. For the enrollment phase, the
system uses k-shot utterances randomly selected from the training split of GSC+, where k
represents the number of examples per keyword class available during system setup. The
inference and evaluation phases utilize all utterances from the test split of both GSC+ and
GSC- partitions, creating a comprehensive evaluation scenario that includes both target
keywords and unknown words.
3.1.2
Prediction Score Computation
The prediction score algorithm in the original framework operates through a multi-step
distance computation and normalization process. When a query sample enters the system,
the framework computes n L2 distances between the query embedding and the n target
keyword prototypes. These prototypes are derived by averaging the embeddings of enroll-
ment samples for each keyword class. The system then employs what the authors term
”open-set classifiers” (including openNCM, openMAX, and DProto variants) to generate
an additional distance representing the query’s relationship to non-keyword content.
This additional distance computation introduces a virtual prototype representing all non-
keywords, creating a total of n+1 distances. The system subsequently normalizes these
distances to produce n+1 probability scores, which serve as the final prediction scores for
classification decisions. This normalization step transforms the raw distance measurements
into a probability distribution over all possible classes, including the unknown class.
3.1.3
Performance Metrics
The original evaluation protocol employs three complementary metrics to assess system
performance across different operational aspects. The Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC)
provides a comprehensive measure of the system’s overall ability to distinguish between
target keywords and non-keywords across all possible threshold settings. This metric offers
insight into the fundamental discriminative power of the learned representations.
The authors also introduce a threshold-based evaluation approach, where they fix the False
Acceptance Rate (FAR) at 5 percent, meaning that among non-keyword utterances, only 5
percent are incorrectly accepted as valid keywords. At this operating point, they measure
22


=== PAGE 29 ===
two additional metrics: classification accuracy within the target keyword set (how well
the system differentiates between different known keywords) and the False Rejection Rate
(how frequently genuine keyword utterances are incorrectly classified as non-keywords).
3.2
Proposed Improvements
Building upon our analysis of the existing methodology, we propose several fundamental
improvements that address key limitations while maintaining the core principles of few-
shot open-set learning. These improvements aim to create a more realistic, generalizable,
and interpretable evaluation framework.
3.2.1
Direct Distance-Based Classification
Our first major improvement involves eliminating the probability normalization step and
utilizing L2 distances directly for classification decisions. This modification addresses
several conceptual and practical limitations in the original approach while providing more
intuitive threshold interpretation.
The fundamental issue with probability-based scoring lies in its dependency on the ”un-
known” distance computation. Consider a scenario where we have five keyword prototypes,
and a query sample produces distances of [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] to these prototypes respectively. If
a second query sample generates distances of [2, 4, 6, 8, 10] (exactly double the first sam-
ple’s distances), the normalized probability scores would be identical without the unknown
distance computation. This mathematical artifact obscures important information about the
absolute positioning of query samples in the embedding space.
The unknown distance serves as a normalization factor that helps differentiate between
queries that are generally close to all prototypes versus those that are distant from all proto-
types. However, this approach introduces artificial complexity and makes threshold inter-
pretation less intuitive. In our direct distance approach, we set acceptance thresholds that
can be understood as acceptance radii around each keyword prototype. A threshold cali-
brated to achieve 5 percent FAR can be interpreted as the radius within which we accept a
query as belonging to a specific keyword class.
This geometric interpretation provides clearer insights into system behavior and removes
the dependency on computing virtual non-keyword representations. Non-keywords are
naturally handled through the rejection mechanism when queries fall outside all acceptance
23


=== PAGE 30 ===
radii, rather than requiring explicit prototype computation.
3.2.2
Mean Detection Error Tradeoff Curves
Our second major improvement replaces the three-metric evaluation approach with a com-
prehensive visualization method based on mean Detection Error Tradeoff (DET) curves.
Traditional evaluation approaches using AUC, accuracy, and false rejection rate provide
limited insight into system behavior across different operating conditions and fail to cap-
ture the complexity of multi-class keyword spotting performance.
The conventional ROC curve and its associated AUC metric focus exclusively on the bi-
nary discrimination between all keywords collectively versus all non-keywords. While this
provides useful information about overall system performance, it fails to capture how well
the system distinguishes between individual keyword classes, which is crucial for practical
deployment scenarios.
Our approach computes individual DET curves for each target keyword class, treating each
keyword against all other content (including both other keywords and non-keywords) as a
separate binary classification problem. This methodology provides detailed insights into
per-class performance while maintaining sensitivity to both false acceptance and false re-
jection characteristics.
The mean DET curve is obtained by averaging the individual DET curves across all target
keyword classes, similar to the approach used in multi-class confusion matrix averaging.
This averaging process preserves the detailed performance characteristics while providing
a single, comprehensive performance summary that captures both keyword discrimination
and rejection capabilities.
Each individual DET curve visualizes the tradeoff between false acceptance and false re-
jection for a specific keyword, revealing how well the system can distinguish that particu-
lar keyword from all other possible audio content. The resulting mean curve provides an
overall performance assessment that naturally incorporates both the accuracy of keyword
classification and the effectiveness of non-keyword rejection.
3.2.3
Enhanced Data Utilization Framework
Our third improvement addresses the lack of generalizability in the original data utilization
strategy by introducing randomized word selection and expanding the evaluation dataset.
24


=== PAGE 31 ===
The original approach uses fixed word partitions across all evaluation runs, which limits the
protocol’s ability to simulate real-world deployment scenarios where users define arbitrary
keyword sets.
We formalize our data utilization terminology to ensure clarity and reproducibility. The
utterance splits used for enrollment and evaluation are termed the enrollment split and
evaluation split respectively. The words selected as targets for the KWS system constitute
the positive partition, while words chosen to represent non-keywords form the negative par-
tition. This terminology provides a clear framework for describing different experimental
configurations.
Our improved protocol introduces randomization in word selection for both positive and
negative partitions across different evaluation runs. This randomization better simulates
real-world scenarios where users can define arbitrary keyword sets, providing a more robust
assessment of system generalizability. The variability in word selection also helps identify
potential biases in the evaluation protocol that might arise from using fixed word sets.
3.2.4
Multi-Lingual Spoken Words Corpus Integration
To address the limited vocabulary size of the GSC dataset (35 words), we propose incor-
porating a subset of the Multi-lingual Spoken Words Corpus (MSWC) for evaluation pur-
poses. The GSC dataset’s small vocabulary limits the introduction of true randomness in
word selection, potentially leading to overfitting of evaluation protocols to specific acoustic
or linguistic characteristics.
Our MSWC subset selection criteria ensure both data quality and experimental validity. We
include words with a minimum of 1000 utterances to guarantee sufficient data for robust
evaluation, while excluding the first 500 words with the highest utterance counts, as these
were used for training the feature encoder in the original study [4]. This exclusion prevents
data leakage and ensures fair evaluation of few-shot learning capabilities. The resulting
dataset contains 263 distinct words, providing substantially more vocabulary diversity than
the original 35-word GSC set.
The utterance splitting strategy for MSWC differs significantly from GSC to optimize eval-
uation effectiveness. We employ a 1:9 ratio between enrollment and evaluation splits, con-
trasting with GSC’s 8:1 ratio. This allocation strategy recognizes that few-shot learning
scenarios require only k samples per keyword for enrollment, making large enrollment
splits inefficient. The emphasis on evaluation data maximizes our ability to assess system
25


=== PAGE 32 ===
performance across diverse acoustic conditions and speaking styles.
For experimental configuration, we randomly select 5 words for the positive partition and
50 words for the negative partition from the 263 available words. The choice of 5 keywords
reflects practical deployment scenarios where systems typically handle a small number of
user-defined commands. The 1:10 ratio between positive and negative partition sizes pro-
vides sufficient negative examples for robust open-set evaluation while maintaining com-
putational feasibility.
3.3
Experimental Design
We design four systematic experiments to validate our proposed improvements and demon-
strate their effectiveness over existing approaches. Each experiment addresses a specific
aspect of our methodology while building toward a comprehensive evaluation of the en-
hanced framework.
3.3.1
Direct Distance vs. Probability-Based Classification
This experiment provides a controlled comparison between our direct L2 distance approach
and the probability-based scoring method originally proposed by Rusci et al. [4]. We repli-
cate the best-performing system configuration reported in their work, maintaining identical
feature encoders, training procedures, and datasets. The only modification is the replace-
ment of probability normalization with a direct distance thresholding strategy. Further-
more, we evaluate the results using both conventional metrics for consistency with prior
literature and the newly proposed metrics to demonstrate its improved interpretability.
This controlled design isolates the effect of our scoring modification from other experi-
mental factors. We hypothesize that removing the artificial constraints introduced by the
”unknown” prototype and probability normalization will lead to superior performance. To
ensure fairness, distance thresholds are calibrated to match equivalent operating points with
the baseline system.
3.3.2
Data Utilization Strategy Comparison
This experiment compares three data utilization strategies arranged by increasing difficulty
and real-world relevance. GSC Fixed uses the original predetermined word selections

across all runs, providing maximum control but limited generalizability. GSC Random-

partitions, introducing deployment-realistic variability. MSWC employs our proposed ex-

We hypothesize that difficulty increases sequentially across these strategies, with each ap-
proach revealing system limitations that simpler evaluations miss. This progressive analysis
demonstrates that our improvements address genuine evaluation limitations rather than in-
troducing artificial complexity. The experiment includes statistical analysis of performance
distributions across randomization runs, examining both mean performance and variance
characteristics.
3.3.3
Few-Shot Performance Scaling
The final experiment characterizes how system performance varies across different few-
shot scenarios, examining the relationship between enrollment data availability and effec-

few-shot learning spectrum from extremely limited to generous enrollment conditions.
Using our enhanced evaluation framework across all k-shot scenarios, we generate mean
DET curves for each condition to reveal how overall performance and the balance between
false acceptance and rejection evolve with data availability. The analysis includes com-
parative examination across all three data utilization strategies from Experiment 3, provid-
ing multi-dimensional insights into few-shot learning scaling characteristics and evaluation
methodology sensitivity.
The experiment extends beyond accuracy measurements to examine stability and reliability,
identifying minimum data requirements for stable performance and diminishing returns
points where additional enrollment examples provide minimal improvements. This analysis
bridges methodological validation with practical deployment guidance, offering actionable
insights for optimizing enrollment burden versus system performance.

5.
Conclusions & Future works
This study introduces an enhanced evaluation framework for few-shot open-set keyword
spotting systems that addresses critical limitations in existing testing protocols. Through
systematic experimental validation, we demonstrate three key methodological improve-
ments that provide more rigorous and realistic assessment of system performance.
Our direct L2 distance-based classification approach eliminates the artificial constraints
introduced by probability normalization and virtual prototype computation, resulting in
measurable performance improvements and more intuitive threshold interpretation. The
geometric understanding of acceptance radii around keyword prototypes provides clearer
insights into system behavior compared to probability-based scoring methods.
The comprehensive evaluation using mean Detection Error Tradeoff curves reveals per-
class performance characteristics that conventional metrics fail to capture. This approach
naturally incorporates both keyword discrimination accuracy and unknown rejection ef-
fectiveness, providing a more complete assessment of open-set classification performance
across different operating conditions.
The enhanced data utilization framework, incorporating randomized word selection and
expanded vocabulary through MSWC integration, exposes significant performance varia-
tions that fixed evaluation protocols mask. Our results demonstrate that systems appearing
highly effective under controlled conditions may struggle considerably in realistic deploy-
ment scenarios, highlighting the importance of rigorous evaluation methodologies.
The few-shot performance scaling analysis reveals that practical systems can achieve rea-
sonable performance with 3-5 enrollment samples per keyword, with diminishing returns
beyond this range. This finding validates the few-shot learning paradigm for keyword
spotting applications while providing actionable guidance for balancing enrollment bur-
den against system effectiveness.

5.1
Future Works
Multi-Modal Evaluation Frameworks: The current evaluation focuses on audio-only
keyword spotting, but real-world deployment scenarios often involve multi-modal contexts.
Extending the evaluation framework to incorporate visual cues, environmental context, or
user behavior patterns could provide more comprehensive system assessment and reveal
additional performance limitations.
Computational Efficiency Analysis: While this study focuses on classification accuracy
and evaluation methodology, practical deployment requires consideration of computational
constraints. Future work should integrate efficiency metrics into the evaluation framework,
examining the tradeoffs between performance improvements and computational overhead,
particularly for edge device deployment.
Personalization and Adaptation: The current framework treats all users equivalently,
but real-world systems may benefit from user-specific adaptations. Investigating how the
evaluation methodology can assess personalization capabilities and long-term adaptation
performance could provide valuable insights for practical deployment scenarios.
Cross-Language Generalization: Although we introduce MSWC for expanded vocab-
ulary evaluation, systematic investigation of cross-language generalization capabilities re-
mains unexplored. Future studies should examine how systems trained on specific language
combinations perform when deployed in multilingual environments, potentially revealing
important generalization limitations.
Integration with Real-Time Processing: The current methodology focuses on offline
evaluation, but practical systems require real-time processing capabilities. Future work
should extend the evaluation framework to assess performance under real-time constraints,
including latency requirements and streaming audio processing challenges.
These research directions collectively aim to bridge the gap between laboratory evaluation
and practical deployment, ensuring that few-shot open-set keyword spotting systems can
reliably serve users in diverse real-world conditions while maintaining the rigorous evalu-
ation standards established in this work.
